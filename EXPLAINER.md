# A plain-language tour (no equations required)

This page explains what this repository does for readers who are not numerical analysts:
teachers, classmates, family, anyone curious. The technical write-ups live in
`report/report.md` and `non-linear/report/report.md`; everything there is also true here,
just said with fewer symbols.

## The problem, in one paragraph

Many things engineers care about are described by *fields*: the temperature at every point
of a metal plate, for example. Computers find these fields by cutting the plate into
thousands of small cells and solving a large system of equations, once per scenario. That
is accurate but slow, and design work needs *thousands* of scenarios: what if the material
conducts heat differently, what if the heater is stronger, what if the coating changes?
This project builds a fast "stand-in" (a **surrogate**) that answers those what-ifs in a
fraction of a millisecond, and, crucially, shows how to build it so we can *trust* it.

## The main idea: a few shapes are enough

Here is the trick, and it is the whole thesis of the project. If you solve the equations
for many different scenarios and stack the answers side by side, the solutions turn out to
be surprisingly repetitive. Almost every temperature field is just a mix of a *few basic
shapes*: an average shape, plus a little more of shape one, a little less of shape two.
Mathematics has a tool (the singular value decomposition, the same engine behind image
compression) that extracts those shapes automatically and even tells you how many matter.

So instead of teaching a big neural network to predict the temperature in every one of
~1,700 cells, we teach a *tiny* network to predict a handful of mixing amounts, the
"sliders" for the few shapes, and rebuild the full picture from those. Like describing a
face as "the average face, plus 30% of variation A, minus 10% of variation B" instead of
listing every pixel. The heavy lifting is done by choosing the right shapes; the network's
job becomes small and easy.

## The two projects in this repository

**1. The square plate (the original Treball de Recerca).** A heated square plate whose
material properties depend on two dials. Result: the tiny slider-predicting network is
about ten times more accurate than a big network predicting every cell, uses about
thirteen times fewer learnable numbers, and its answers respect the physics far better.
A surprise worth remembering: giving the method *more* shapes eventually makes it *worse*,
because the extra sliders are so faint that the network ends up learning noise.

**2. The space radiator (`non-linear/`).** Computers in orbit can only get rid of heat by
glowing it away as radiation; there is no air in space. Radiation is a genuinely harder
kind of physics (the heat loss grows with the *fourth power* of temperature), and it is
the exact problem faced by the new generation of orbital data centres. We simulate a 2D
radiator panel with an electronics patch that switches on and off like a satellite's duty
cycle, and ask the same question. Two findings:

- When the heater stays in one place, the steady temperature fields are mixes of just
  **three** shapes, to within computer precision. All the hard nonlinearity hides in *how
  much* of each shape you need, which is exactly the part the little network learns.
- When the electronics blink on and off, the story gets richer: the changing fields need
  more shapes (roughly 6 to 12), and the surrogate has to track time too. It does, well
  enough for its real job; see next.

**The pay-off test.** We asked both the slow, trusted simulator and the fast surrogate the
same engineering question: across 100 combinations of coating and duty cycle, which design
keeps the chip coolest? They returned essentially the same ranking (rank agreement 0.999),
the same best design, and the surrogate was hundreds to thousands of times faster. That is
what makes this a *design tool* and not just a demo.

## Why you can trust the numbers

This is the part of the project we are proudest of, and it needs no advanced mathematics
to appreciate:

- **Known answers first.** Before any machine learning, the simulator is checked against
  problems whose exact answer we know on paper. It reproduces them, and its error shrinks
  at exactly the textbook rate when the grid is refined.
- **Energy bookkeeping.** Watts in must equal watts out. Our simulations balance to about
  one part in a hundred billion.
- **No cherry-picking.** Every headline number is regenerated by numbered scripts anyone
  can run; we retrained the networks from five different random starting points and report
  the spread, not the best case; and we state plainly where the surrogate fails (step
  outside the range it was trained on and errors grow about seventeen-fold).
- **Automatic re-checking.** A test suite with dozens of checks runs on every change.

## What this does *not* claim

The plates are 2D rectangles, the geometry never changes, and the surrogate is only
trustworthy inside the range of scenarios it saw during training. This is a carefully
controlled prototype that demonstrates a principle, not a certified spacecraft tool.

## Tiny glossary

- **PDE (partial differential equation):** the mathematical rulebook saying how heat
  spreads.
- **Full-order model (FOM):** the slow, trusted, cell-by-cell simulation.
- **Surrogate:** the fast stand-in trained to imitate it.
- **POD / reduced basis:** the automatically-found "few basic shapes".
- **Coefficients:** the slider positions saying how much of each shape to mix in.
- **Residual:** plug an answer back into the rulebook and measure how badly it disobeys;
  small residual means the physics is respected.
- **Emissivity (ε):** how good a surface is at glowing heat away (0 = mirror, 1 = perfect
  radiator).
- **Duty cycle:** the fraction of time the electronics are switched on.
